Asperger's Syndrome is a neurodevelopmental disorder that was formerly a distinct diagnosis but is now considered part of the broader Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) in the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM-5). While it's no longer a separate diagnosis, many people and some providers still use the term, often referring to it as "ASD â€“ without intellectual or language impairment" or "high-functioning autism." Individuals with Asperger's typically have average or above-average intelligence and no significant language delays in terms of vocabulary or grammar. However, they experience challenges primarily in social communication and interaction, alongside restricted interests and repetitive behaviors.

Fact File

  • Formerly a distinct diagnosis, now part of Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) in the DSM-5.
  • Often described as high-functioning ASD or ASD without intellectual or language impairment.
  • Social Communication and Interaction Challenges:
    • Difficulty understanding and using nonverbal cues (eye contact, facial expressions, body language, gestures).
    • Challenges with social reciprocity (back-and-back conversation, sharing interests/emotions).
    • May struggle to make friends or understand social rules.
    • Speech might be unusual (monotone, overly formal, loud, or choppy).
    • May "talk at" people rather than "to" them, focusing on their intense interests.
  • Restricted, Repetitive Patterns of Behavior, Interests, or Activities:
    • Intense, highly focused interests in specific topics.
    • Inflexible adherence to routines or rituals; resistance to change.
    • Repetitive motor mannerisms (e.g., hand flapping, toe walking).
    • Unusual reactions to sensory input (hypersensitivity or hyposensitivity to light, sound, textures, etc.).
  • Typically average or above-average intelligence.
  • Generally no significant delay in spoken or receptive language development (vocabulary, grammar).
  • However, challenges may exist in the *social use* of language (pragmatics).
  • Symptoms typically emerge in early childhood.
  • The exact causes are unknown, but it's believed to involve a combination of genetic and environmental factors.
  • It is not caused by upbringing or vaccines.
  • No specific medical tests (like blood tests or imaging scans).
  • Diagnosis is made through comprehensive assessments by a team of health professionals, observing behavior and gathering information on developmental history and current functioning.
  • Assessment includes looking at social and emotional abilities, communication skills, learning abilities, movement skills, and special interests.
  • Can be diagnosed in childhood, though some individuals are diagnosed in adulthood.
  • There is no "cure," but various therapies and interventions can help individuals manage symptoms and function to their highest potential.
  • Common approaches include:
    • Social skills training: To improve social interaction and understanding of social cues.
    • Speech and language therapy: To address pragmatic language difficulties.
    • Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT): To help manage emotions, anxiety, and repetitive behaviors.
    • Occupational therapy: For sensory integration issues and fine/gross motor skills.
    • Physical therapy: To address motor coordination difficulties.
    • Parent/family training and support: To equip caregivers with strategies.
    • Medication: May be used to treat co-occurring conditions like anxiety, depression, or ADHD.
  • Treatment is individualized and focuses on building on the individual's strengths while addressing challenges.

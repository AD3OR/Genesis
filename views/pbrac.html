Brachydactyly is a medical term that literally means "short digits" (brachy = short, dactylos = finger/toe). It's a condition characterized by abnormally short fingers and/or toes. It is often inherited and can occur as an isolated anomaly or as part of a more complex syndrome. There are several types of brachydactyly, classified based on which bones of the fingers or toes are shortened. This condition can range in severity and may affect one or multiple digits.

Fact File

  • Abnormally short fingers and/or toes.
  • Can affect one or more digits.
  • Often inherited, can be isolated or part of a syndrome.
  • Various types (A1, A2, B, C, D, E) based on affected bones.
  • Shortening of phalanges (finger/toe bones) or metacarpals/metatarsals (hand/foot bones).
  • Can range from mild (e.g., short thumb) to severe (all digits affected).
  • May involve specific digits (e.g., middle phalanx of index finger).
  • Often symmetrical, but can be unilateral.
  • May be associated with other skeletal abnormalities.
  • Primarily genetic, typically inherited in an autosomal dominant pattern.
  • Caused by mutations in specific genes involved in bone development (e.g., GDF5, IHH, PTHLH, HOXD13).
  • Can occur spontaneously (de novo mutation).
  • Can be a feature of broader genetic syndromes (e.g., Down syndrome, Apert syndrome, Rubinstein-Taybi syndrome).
  • Clinical examination (visual inspection, measurement of digits).
  • X-rays to confirm bone shortening and identify affected bones.
  • Family history assessment.
  • Genetic testing may be performed to identify specific gene mutations, especially if part of a syndrome.
  • Often diagnosed at birth or in early childhood.
  • No specific "cure" for the genetic condition itself.
  • Treatment is usually not medically necessary unless there's functional impairment or significant cosmetic concern.
  • Common approaches include:
    • Occupational/Physical Therapy: To improve hand/foot function if mobility is affected.
    • Surgery: Rarely considered, primarily for functional improvement (e.g., increasing grip) or significant cosmetic reasons. More common in severe cases or when associated with other deformities.
    • Genetic Counseling: Important for affected individuals and families to understand inheritance patterns and recurrence risks.
    • Focus on adaptation and maximizing function.
